National Parks FAQ

How many people visit the national parks?
Total recreation visitors to the national parks in 2018: 318,211,833.
How old is the National Park System?
The National Park Service was created by an act signed by President Woodrow Wilson on August 25, 1916.
How many employees are in the National Park Service?
Permanent, temporary, and seasonal employees: More than 20,000
What is the origin of the National Park Service arrowhead?
The arrowhead was authorized as the official National Park Service emblem by the Secretary of the Interior on July 20, 1951.
How many areas are in the National Park System?
The system includes 419 areas covering more than 85 million acres in every state, the District of Columbia, American Samoa, Guam, Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands.
What is the largest national park site?
Wrangell-St. Elias National Park and Preserve, AK, at 13.2 million acres.

Before you head out, check with the national parks that you intend to visit. In many national parks, off-road driving is illegal. Where off-road driving is allowed, the National Park Service regulates it.

Lassen Volcanic

  • Lassen Volcanic National Park is home to an array of geothermal features and is known for its active geothermal areas, including boiling springs, fumaroles, and the famous Bumpass Hell, a hydrothermal area with boiling springs and fumaroles.
  • The park is named after Lassen Peak, which is the southernmost active volcano in the Cascade Range. It last erupted in 1915, and the devastation caused by the eruption is still visible in some areas of the park.
  • Despite its volcanic landscape, Lassen Volcanic National Park boasts diverse ecosystems, including coniferous forests, meadows, and lakes. The park is home to a variety of plant and animal species adapted to the challenging conditions.
  • Lassen Volcanic National Park has been designated as a Dark Sky Sanctuary, making it an excellent location for stargazing. The absence of light pollution allows visitors to enjoy the night sky and celestial events.

Mammoth Cave

  • Mammoth Cave National Park is home to the world's longest known cave system, with over 400 miles (640 kilometers) of explored passageways. The cave system features impressive stalactites, stalagmites, and other unique geological formations.
  • The cave has a rich history and has been used by Native Americans, early European settlers, and even as a source of saltpeter during the War of 1812 and the Civil War. The cave was established as a national park in 1941.
  • While the underground cave system is the main attraction, Mammoth Cave National Park also features diverse surface habitats, including rivers, hills, and forests. The park is home to a variety of plant and animal species.
  • Mammoth Cave National Park has been designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing its outstanding cultural and natural significance.

Wind Cave

  • Wind Cave is known for its complex and extensive network of underground passages, making it the world's fourth-longest cave. It features unique boxwork formations, which are thin blades of calcite that form intricate patterns on cave walls.
  • The park is home to a herd of American bison, a species that was once on the brink of extinction. The reintroduction of bison to Wind Cave National Park has been a successful conservation effort.
  • Wind Cave was first designated a national park in 1903, making it one of the earliest national parks in the United States. Its creation was spurred by the desire to protect the unique cave formations and the surrounding ecosystem.
  • Above ground, the park features a mixed-grass prairie ecosystem, providing habitat for a variety of wildlife, including prairie dogs, elk, and coyotes. The diverse landscape adds to the park's ecological significance.
